Freight Class & Dimensional Weight

Ever wondered why a box of pillows can cost more to ship than a box of books? The answer is chargeable weight. Here's how carriers really price your cargo.

By the ShippingAndLogistics team 10 July 2026 6 min read

Freight pricing surprises a lot of first-time shippers. You weigh your pallet, expect to pay for those kilos, and then the invoice charges you for far more. The reason is that carriers don't just care how heavy your shipment is — they care how much space it takes up. That's where dimensional weight comes in.

Key takeaway
Carriers charge on chargeable weight — the greater of your shipment's actual weight and its volumetric (dimensional) weight. Bulky, lightweight cargo is priced on the space it occupies.

Why space matters as much as weight

A truck or aircraft runs out of room long before it runs out of weight capacity when it's carrying light, bulky goods. To price fairly, carriers convert the volume of your shipment into a notional weight — the volumetric weight — and bill you on whichever is higher: actual or volumetric.

How to calculate volumetric weight

The formula is straightforward. Multiply the dimensions to get volume, then divide by a "dim factor" set by the carrier and mode:

Volumetric weight (kg) = (Length × Width × Height in cm) ÷ dim factor

The dim factor differs by mode. Common values are:

ModeTypical dim factor
Air freight6,000
Road freight (courier/parcel)5,000
Express couriers5,000 (often)

A worked example

Say you're air-freighting a carton measuring 120 × 80 × 100 cm that weighs 60 kg:

  • Volume = 120 × 80 × 100 = 960,000 cm³
  • Volumetric weight = 960,000 ÷ 6,000 = 160 kg
  • Actual weight = 60 kg

The carrier bills the chargeable weight of 160 kg, not 60 kg — because your light carton is taking up the space of a 160 kg load. This is exactly why bulky-but-light goods feel expensive to fly.

Sea freight works a little differently

For less-than-container-load (LCL) ocean freight, the equivalent concept is weight or measure (W/M). Carriers compare your cargo's weight in tonnes against its volume in cubic metres and charge on whichever is greater, where 1 cubic metre is treated as 1,000 kg. Full-container-load (FCL) shipments are priced per container, so dimensional weight doesn't apply — you're paying for the box whether it's full or half-empty.

What is freight class?

You'll mainly see freight class referenced on North American road freight (the NMFC system), where goods are grouped into classes from 50 to 500 based on density, handling, stowability and liability. Denser, easier-to-handle goods get a lower class and a lower rate. While it's not used in the same formal way for UK domestic movements, the underlying principle is identical everywhere: the denser your cargo, the better your rate per kilo.

How to lower your chargeable weight

A few practical habits keep your bills down:

  • Reduce empty space. Right-size your packaging — oversized boxes inflate volumetric weight.
  • Improve density. Where safe, consolidate items so pallets are well-filled.
  • Measure accurately. Round up honestly; carriers re-measure and re-bill discrepancies (often with a fee).
  • Palletise efficiently. Stack to standard pallet footprints and avoid overhang.
  • Ask about the dim factor before booking so you can predict the chargeable weight.

Understanding chargeable weight also makes it much easier to compare freight quotes accurately — because you can check that every provider is measuring your cargo the same way.
