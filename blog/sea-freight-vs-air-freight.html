Sea Freight vs Air Freight

Four trade-offs decide whether your cargo should sail or fly: cost, speed, reliability and carbon. Here's how to weigh them for your shipment.

By the ShippingAndLogistics team 24 June 2026 7 min read

For most international shipments, the first big decision is the mode: ocean or air. It shapes your cost, your lead time and even how much stock you need to hold. The good news is that the choice usually comes down to four factors — and once you've weighed them, the answer is often obvious.

The short version
Choose sea freight for large, heavy or non-urgent cargo where cost matters most. Choose air freight for small, high-value or time-critical goods where speed and reliability justify the premium.

1. Cost

Sea freight is dramatically cheaper per kilogram — often 4 to 6 times less than air for the same weight. Ocean carriers price by container (a full 20ft or 40ft box, known as FCL) or by volume for smaller loads (LCL, or less-than-container-load). Air freight is priced on chargeable weight, which is the greater of actual weight and volumetric weight, so bulky-but-light cargo is punished heavily in the air.

As a rough guide: the heavier and bulkier your shipment, the more sea freight pulls ahead on cost.

2. Speed

This is where air wins decisively. A typical comparison on a long-haul lane looks like this:

ModeTypical door-to-door transit
Air freight2–8 days
Sea freight (FCL)25–45 days
Sea freight (LCL)30–50 days (extra consolidation time)

If a delay costs you a sale, a production stoppage or a missed season, air freight's premium can pay for itself many times over.

3. Reliability and risk

Air services generally run to tighter, more predictable schedules and see far less handling, which lowers the risk of damage. Ocean freight is more exposed to port congestion, blank sailings and weather — though a well-planned sea shipment with buffer time is perfectly dependable. Consider your tolerance for variability, not just the average transit time.

4. Carbon footprint

Air freight emits vastly more CO₂ per tonne-kilometre than ocean — by some estimates 20 to 50 times more. If sustainability reporting or customer expectations matter to your business, sea freight is the clear low-carbon choice, and it's worth quantifying the difference in your emissions reporting.

What about a middle ground?

You don't always have to pick one. Two hybrid options are worth knowing:

  • Sea-air — cargo travels by ocean to a hub, then flies the final leg. Cheaper than pure air, faster than pure sea.
  • Rail freight — on Asia–Europe lanes, rail sits neatly between the two on both cost and speed.

A quick decision checklist

Lean towards air if:

  • Your goods are urgent, perishable or seasonal.
  • The cargo is small and high-value (electronics, pharma, samples).
  • The cost of holding stock or missing a deadline is high.

Lean towards sea if:

  • Your shipment is large, heavy or bulky.
  • You have a predictable lead time and can plan ahead.
  • Cost efficiency and lower emissions are the priority.
