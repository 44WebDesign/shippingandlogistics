How to Compare Freight Quotes Like a Pro

The cheapest headline rate is rarely the cheapest shipment. Here's how to read a freight quote properly, spot the hidden charges, and compare providers on a genuine like-for-like basis.

By the ShippingAndLogistics team 10 June 2026 8 min read

Ask three freight forwarders for a quote on the same shipment and you'll often get three wildly different numbers. That's not because one is ripping you off — it's usually because each quote bundles a different set of services, surcharges and assumptions. Compare them on headline price alone and you can easily pick the option that ends up costing you the most once the invoices land.

This guide walks through exactly what sits inside a freight quote, the charges that catch shippers out, and a simple framework for comparing quotes fairly.

Key takeaway
Always compare quotes on the total landed cost — origin charges, main carriage, destination charges, customs and surcharges combined — not the freight rate in isolation.

Start with the same Incoterm

Before you compare anything, make sure every quote is priced against the same Incoterm. Incoterms are the internationally recognised rules that define where the seller's responsibility ends and yours begins. A quote priced EXW (Ex Works) looks far cheaper than one priced DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) — but that's because EXW excludes export clearance, main freight, import duty and final delivery, all of which you'll pay for separately.

The most common terms you'll see are:

  • EXW — you arrange everything from the supplier's door.
  • FOB (Free On Board) — the supplier gets goods onto the vessel; you cover main freight onwards.
  • CIF (Cost, Insurance & Freight) — the supplier covers freight and insurance to the destination port; you handle import clearance and delivery.
  • DDP — the supplier (or forwarder) delivers to your door with duties paid.

If you're comparing a FOB quote against a DDP quote, you're comparing apples with oranges. Fix the Incoterm first.

Break every quote into its real components

A complete freight quote has more moving parts than most people expect. Lay each quote out against these buckets so nothing hides:

Cost bucketWhat it covers
Origin chargesExport customs, haulage to port, terminal handling, documentation
Main carriageThe ocean, air or road freight rate itself
SurchargesFuel (BAF), currency (CAF), peak season, security, low-sulphur fuel
Destination chargesTerminal handling, delivery order, haulage to final address
Customs & dutyImport declaration, duty, import VAT, inspection fees
InsuranceMarine cargo cover for loss or damage in transit

A quote that only shows "main carriage" isn't wrong — but it isn't complete. Ask every provider to itemise all six buckets.

Watch for the charges that catch people out

These are the line items that most often turn a "cheap" quote into an expensive one:

  • Terminal Handling Charges (THC) at both ends — easily £150–£300 per container.
  • Bunker Adjustment Factor (BAF) — a fuel surcharge that moves with oil prices.
  • Demurrage and detention — charged when a container sits at the port or in your yard longer than the free time allows.
  • Customs examination fees — if your shipment is selected for inspection.
  • Amendment fees — for correcting paperwork after it's been submitted.
A rule of thumb: if one quote is dramatically cheaper than the others, assume it's missing something. Ask what's excluded before you celebrate.

Compare transit time and reliability, not just price

Two ocean services on the same route can differ by 10–15 days. A slower service with a transhipment might save you £200 but tie up your cash in stock for two extra weeks. For time-sensitive goods, factor in the cost of the delay itself. It's also worth asking about the carrier's schedule reliability — the percentage of sailings that arrive on time — which varies enormously between shipping lines.

Check what's included beyond the numbers

Service quality is part of the price. Before you decide, ask each provider:

  • Is customs clearance handled in-house or subcontracted?
  • Will you have a named point of contact, or a ticket queue?
  • What tracking and milestone updates are provided?
  • How are exceptions (rolled cargo, delays, inspections) communicated?

A simple comparison framework

Put your quotes side by side and score each on four axes:

  1. Total landed cost — every bucket added together, same Incoterm.
  2. Transit time — door to door, including customs.
  3. Reliability — on-time performance and carrier reputation.
  4. Service — communication, support and clearance capability.

The best-value option is the one that wins across all four — which is frequently not the lowest sticker price.
