12 Proven Ways to Cut Your Shipping Costs

Freight is one of the most controllable line items in your supply chain — if you know where to look. These twelve tactics take real money off your international shipping spend.

By the ShippingAndLogistics team 18 July 2026 8 min read

Shipping costs can feel fixed — a price the market hands you. In reality, a large share of your freight spend is within your control. The savings rarely come from one big move; they come from stacking several smaller ones. Here are twelve that consistently work.

Before you start
You can't cut what you can't see. Get a clear picture of your total landed cost per shipment first — our guide on comparing freight quotes shows you how.

1. Consolidate your shipments

Shipping four small pallets separately is almost always dearer than shipping them together. Consolidate orders into fewer, fuller movements — combining LCL loads or filling a full container (FCL) — and your cost per unit drops sharply.

2. Fill the container you're paying for

If you're already booking an FCL, a half-empty box is money wasted — you pay for the container, not the contents. Plan order volumes so containers travel as full as is safe and practical.

3. Choose the right Incoterm

Buying EXW or FOB and arranging your own freight often beats letting the supplier ship CIF or DDP with a margin baked into their rate. Controlling the main carriage yourself gives you rate visibility and negotiating power.

4. Book off-peak where you can

Ocean rates swing with demand. Peak-season surcharges bite hardest before major holidays and retail seasons. If your goods aren't urgent, shipping outside peak windows can save a meaningful percentage.

5. Right-size your packaging

Because carriers bill on dimensional weight, oversized boxes mean you're paying to ship air. Match packaging to product and improve density to lower your chargeable weight.

6. Give more lead time

Last-minute shipments push you towards expensive air freight and premium express services. Better forecasting lets you use slower, cheaper ocean or road options — the single biggest lever for many shippers.

7. Use Postponed VAT Accounting

It won't cut the freight rate, but for VAT-registered UK importers, PVA keeps import VAT off your immediate cash outflow, improving working capital. See our customs clearance guide for how it works.

8. Classify goods correctly

The right commodity code ensures you don't overpay duty — and avoids costly delays and penalties from getting it wrong. Where a trade agreement applies, valid proof of origin can reduce duty to zero.

9. Compare carriers and routes regularly

Rates and service quality shift constantly. A carrier that was competitive last year may not be now. Re-quoting periodically — or using a forwarder who does it for you — keeps you on the best available rate.

10. Avoid demurrage and detention

These charges accrue when containers sit too long at the port or in your yard. Have paperwork ready, clear customs promptly, and plan collection so you stay inside the free time. Held shipments are pure avoidable cost.

11. Insure smartly, not blindly

Marine cargo insurance is cheap relative to the value it protects, but make sure you're covering the right value and not doubling up with cover your supplier already provides. One appropriate policy beats several overlapping ones.

12. Build a relationship with one forwarder

Volume and loyalty earn better rates, priority space in tight markets, and a team that understands your cargo. Spreading tiny volumes across many providers usually costs more than consolidating with a partner who'll fight for your rate.

None of these tactics is dramatic on its own. Stack six or seven of them and a 10–20% reduction in total freight spend is realistic for most shippers.
