UK Import Customs Clearance Explained

EORI numbers, commodity codes, import duty and VAT can feel like a maze. This plain-English guide covers what you need — and in what order — to get goods across the UK border.

By the ShippingAndLogistics team 2 July 2026 9 min read

Customs clearance is the process of getting official permission to bring goods into the country, declaring what they are, and paying any duty and tax owed. Done right, it's routine. Done wrong, it means delays, storage charges and unexpected bills. Here's what every UK importer should understand.

This article is a general guide, not tax or legal advice. Rates and rules change — always check the current position on GOV.UK or with your customs agent for your specific goods.

Step 1: Get an EORI number

Before you can import commercially, you need an EORI number (Economic Operators Registration and Identification). For UK imports this starts with GB. It's free, you apply through HMRC, and it usually arrives within a few working days. Without it, your goods can't be cleared.

Step 2: Classify your goods with a commodity code

Every product has a commodity code (also called a tariff or HS code) — a 10-digit number for imports that determines the rate of duty, any restrictions, and whether licences are needed. Getting this right is the single most important part of clearance:

  • Under-classify and you risk fines and back-payments.
  • Over-classify and you pay more duty than you owe.

You can look codes up using the UK Trade Tariff tool. If you're unsure, a customs agent or an HMRC Advance Tariff Ruling gives you certainty.

Step 3: Work out duty and import VAT

Two separate charges usually apply on import:

ChargeHow it's calculated
Import dutyA percentage set by the commodity code, applied to the customs value of the goods (typically cost + freight + insurance)
Import VATUsually 20%, charged on the goods value + duty + shipping

Duty is generally not recoverable, but VAT-registered businesses can normally reclaim import VAT. Most importers use Postponed VAT Accounting (PVA), which lets you account for import VAT on your VAT return instead of paying it upfront at the border — a big cash-flow advantage.

Cash-flow tip
If you're VAT-registered, ask your forwarder to use Postponed VAT Accounting. It keeps import VAT off your immediate cash outlay and moves it onto your VAT return.

Step 4: Prepare the right documents

Clearance depends on accurate paperwork. For a typical import you'll need:

  • Commercial invoice — describing the goods, value, currency and parties.
  • Packing list — quantities, weights and dimensions.
  • Bill of lading or air waybill — the transport document from the carrier.
  • Import declaration — submitted to HMRC's CDS (Customs Declaration Service).
  • Certificates or licences — for controlled goods (food, chemicals, electronics, etc.).
  • Proof of origin — to claim reduced duty under a trade agreement, where eligible.

Step 5: Submit the declaration

Import declarations are filed electronically through the Customs Declaration Service (CDS). Most importers don't do this themselves — they appoint a customs agent or freight forwarder to submit on their behalf, because the system and data requirements are technical. You remain legally responsible for the accuracy of the information, so choose an agent who checks rather than just keys in what you send.

Common reasons goods get held

Avoid these and clearance is usually smooth:

  • Incorrect or vague goods descriptions — "samples" or "parts" isn't enough.
  • Mismatched values between the invoice and the declaration.
  • Wrong commodity code triggering a query.
  • Missing licences for restricted goods.
  • No proof of origin when claiming a preferential duty rate.

When goods are held, demurrage and storage charges can build up fast — another reason accurate paperwork pays for itself.

Should you use a customs agent?

For most importers, yes. A good agent classifies goods correctly, files the declaration, applies reliefs like PVA, and resolves queries with HMRC on your behalf. The fee is modest against the cost of a single held shipment. If you import regularly, an in-house-capable forwarder that also handles your freight keeps everything under one roof.
